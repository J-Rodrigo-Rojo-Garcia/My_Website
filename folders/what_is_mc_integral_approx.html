What is a Monte Carlo integration?

Given a function \(f:\mathbb{R}^{d}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), and a probability density \(\rho(x)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^{d}.\) An expectation with respect to \(\rho\) is calculated as follows: $$\mathbb{E}^{\rho}[f] := \int_{\mathbb{R}^{d}}f(x)\rho(x)dx.$$ Sometimes is difficult or even impossible to express the solution of this integral with an analytical expression, then, we need to estimate a numerical solution. A classical numerical approximation is given by quadrature rules (see e.g., [4]). Nervertheless, this method in unfeasible when the dimension \( d \) is high. The reason is the high number of nodes needed for approximate the integral with a reasonable error. Furthermore, the rate of convergence is dependent of the dimension and limitates the convergence to zero of the error. This phenomenon is known as the curse of dimensionality [1].


Another approach based in statistical approximation is given by Monte Carlo methods. This technique consists in calculate an ensemble \(\lbrace X_{k} \rbrace_{k=1}^{M} \) with distribution \(X_{k} \sim \rho \) and where each sample is i.i.d. Then, the expectation is approximated with an average as follows: $$\mathbb{E}^{\rho}[f] \approx \frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M}f(X_{k}).$$

A Monte Carlo approximation has the virtue of be a method independent of the dimension. And the error (rate of convergence) for bounded functions in the unit ball is given by the expression: $$ \sup _{|f|_{\infty} \leq 1}\left(\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_{\mathbb{R}^{d}}f(x)\rho(x)dx-\frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M}f(X_{k})\right)^2\right]\right)^{1 / 2} \leq M^{-1/2}, $$

and in general for bounded functions, the rate of convergence is in the order \(\mathcal{O}(M^{-1/2})\) (see e.g., [5]).


Even when the rate of convergence of this method is slow, it is still considered a powerful tool for estimate integrals, due its inmmunity respect to the dimension. Monte Carlo integration has abundant applications in areas as financial mathematics, stochastic differential equations, computational physics, statistical mechanics, and uncertainty quantification theory (see e.g., [2,3,6]).
