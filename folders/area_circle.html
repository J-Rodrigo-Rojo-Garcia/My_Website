Area of circle

How it works?


A classical example of Monte Carlo integration is the estimation of \(pi\) or the area of a circle with Monte Carlo integration (see e.g., [1]). Now we ilustrate how to implement it for this purpose.


Let a circle be with center in the coordinates \((0,0)\) and radius \(r = 1\). The classical formula of the area is given by the expression \(A = \pi r^{2}\), which results in \(A = \pi \). We can demonstrate this formula using multivariate calculus machinery, in particular the formula can be prooved with the double integral:

$$ A = \int_{B(0,1)}dxdy,$$

where the domain of integration is the unit ball \(B(0,1) = \lbrace (x,y)\in \mathbb{R}^{2} \mid x^{2} + y^{2} < 1 \rbrace \).


Let us notice that the last integral is equivalent to an integral inside the domain \(D := [-1,1] \times [-1,1]\) as follows:

$$ \int_{B(0,1)}dxdy = \int_{D}\mathbb{I}_{B(0,1)}(x,y)dxdy = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{2}}\mathbb{I}_{B(0,1)}(x,y)\mathbb{I}_{D}(x,y)dxdy.$$

Then, the integral can be represented as an expectation of the function \(f(x,y) := \mathbb{I}_{B(0,1)}(x,y)\) with respect to the bidimensional uniform distribution \(\mathcal{U}(D)\), and with density \(\rho(x,y) = \frac{1}{4}\mathbb{I}_{D}(x,y)\), i.e.,

$$ A = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{2}}\mathbb{I}_{B(0,1)}(x,y)\mathbb{I}_{D}(x,y)dxdy = 4\mathbb{E}^{\rho}[f].$$

Now, given an ensemble \(\lbrace X_{k} \rbrace_{k=1}^{M}\) of i.i.d. samples distributed uniform \(\mathcal{U}(D)\), the MC approximation is reduced as follows:

$$ A = 4\mathbb{E}^{\rho}[f] \approx 4\frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M}f(X_{k}^{1},X_{k}^{2}) = 4\frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M}\mathbb{I}_{B(0,1)}(X_{k}^{1},X_{k}^{2}) = 4\frac{N}{M},$$

where \(N \) is the number of samples inside the unit ball.
